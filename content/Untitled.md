## Affect

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Pre-personal, bodily intensities that circulate between bodies and exceed conscious emotion (Massumi 2002; Ahmed 2004). Affect operates as "the autonomy of affect" – transpersonal forces that pass body-to-body before registering as recognizable feelings, generating collective capacities for action and reaction.

**Postqualitative research use** – Affect guides methodological attention to the intensive, visceral dimensions of research encounters. Rather than treating emotions as individual psychological states, postqualitative inquiry tracks how affects move through research assemblages, shaping what becomes possible to know and experience (MacLure 2013).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – Writing instructors experience complex affective encounters with GenAI – anxiety about authenticity, excitement about efficiency, visceral responses to machine-generated text that "feels wrong" or surprisingly resonant. These affects precede cognitive assessment and reshape pedagogical relationships through bodily intensities.

Key citation: Massumi, B. (2002). _Parables for the virtual: Movement, affect, sensation_. Duke University Press.

## Affective Labor

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – The invisible emotional and relational work that produces social bonds, care, and communicative practices (Hardt & Negri 2000). Feminist theorists emphasize how affective labor is often feminized, devalued, and essential to capitalism's reproduction through the production of subjectivity and social cooperation.

**Postqualitative research use** – Postqualitative inquiry attends to the affective labor embedded in research relationships – the emotional work of building trust, managing trauma, sustaining collaborative inquiry. This labor is not separate from knowledge production but constitutive of what becomes knowable (Hickey-Moody & Page 2015).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – Writing instructors perform extensive affective labor in responding to student writing – emotional attunement, encouragement, critique delivered with care. GenAI potentially automates some feedback labor while intensifying others: managing student anxieties about AI detection, teaching ethical AI use, rebuilding trust when AI mediation feels impersonal.

Prompt design example: "Respond to this student draft in a way that acknowledges their effort while suggesting revision possibilities. Consider how your tone might affect their willingness to continue developing these ideas."

## Agential Realism

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Karen Barad's (2007) onto-epistemological framework positing that phenomena emerge through intra-active entanglements rather than existing as discrete entities. Reality is not composed of separate objects and observers but co-constituted through material-discursive practices within specific apparatus of observation.

**Postqualitative research use** – Agential realism reframes research as participatory universe-making rather than discovering pre-existing truths. Researchers, participants, concepts, and materials are co-constituted through specific methodological apparatus, generating phenomena that didn't exist prior to the research encounter (Barad 2007).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – Writing instructors and GenAI don't exist as separate entities that then interact; instead, they emerge as phenomena through specific pedagogical apparatus. The "instructor" and "AI" are co-constituted through platform interfaces, institutional policies, and classroom practices, generating new hybrid agencies in writing instruction.

This approach moves beyond instrumentalist views of technology to examine how human and artificial agencies are mutually constitutive. Writing instructors report feeling "changed" by working with AI – not just in their practices but in their sense of what constitutes teaching, creativity, and pedagogical relationships.

Key citation: Barad, K. (2007). _Meeting the universe halfway: Quantum physics and the entanglement of matter and meaning_. Duke University Press.

## Apparatus

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – In Barad's agential realism, apparatus are material-discursive practices that enact agential cuts, creating separations between "subject" and "object" while generating the phenomena observed. Apparatus are not static instruments but dynamic practices that reconfigure spacetimematter (Barad 2007).

**Postqualitative research use** – Research apparatus include not just methods and instruments but the entire material-discursive assemblage that generates research phenomena: institutional contexts, researcher embodiment, theoretical frameworks, data collection practices, and writing processes (Lenz Taguchi 2012).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – GenAI writing tools function as apparatus that reconfigure pedagogical phenomena, enacting new boundaries between student/machine authorship, original/assisted writing, and human/artificial creativity. These apparatus don't simply measure writing but actively participate in its production and evaluation.

Classroom writing apparatus now include: student bodies, keyboards, AI interfaces, institutional policies, instructor expertise, peer feedback, AI detection tools, and grading rubrics. These elements together enact what counts as "writing" and "learning" in contemporary composition courses.

Research question: How do different GenAI apparatus (ChatGPT, Grammarly, Jasper) enact different writing pedagogies and student subjectivities? What new boundaries and possibilities emerge through these reconfigurations?

## Assemblage

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Dynamic networks of heterogeneous elements (human, nonhuman, discursive, material) that generate capacities for action beyond their constituent parts (Deleuze & Guattari 1980; DeLanda 2016). Assemblages are characterized by relations of exteriority, meaning components can be detached and enter new assemblages.

**Postqualitative research use** – Postqualitative inquiry examines research as assemblage processes where researchers, participants, concepts, affects, and materials connect in ways that exceed individual agency. Methods emerge from assemblage relations rather than being applied to pre-given objects of study (Fox & Alldred 2015).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – Writing pedagogy becomes an assemblage of instructors, students, AI systems, institutional policies, disciplinary traditions, and technological affordances. GenAI doesn't simply get "added" to existing teaching but reconfigures the entire assemblage, generating new capacities for writing, learning, and evaluation.

When instructors report that GenAI "changes everything" about their teaching, they're describing assemblage effects – how introducing AI tools reconfigures the entire network of relationships, generating new affordances and constraints. Some connections strengthen (AI-assisted feedback loops) while others weaken (traditional drafting processes).

Research might map how different assemblage configurations generate different pedagogical outcomes. What happens when AI detection software is added to the assemblage? How do institutional "AI bans" reconfigure student-instructor relationships?

## Atmospheric Attunement

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – The capacity to sense and respond to ambient conditions, moods, and atmospheric qualities that shape social life (Stewart 2011; Anderson 2009). Atmospheres are collective affects that create background conditions for what becomes possible within particular spaces and times.

**Postqualitative research use** – Atmospheric attunement guides methodological sensitivity to the ambient qualities of research encounters – the mood in the room, tensions that circulate, energies that build or dissipate. Research attends to how atmospheres condition what can be said, felt, or known (MacLure 2013).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – GenAI generates new classroom atmospheres – anxieties about surveillance, excitement about efficiency, uncertainties about authenticity. Writing instructors must develop atmospheric attunement to sense how AI technologies reshape the ambient conditions of teaching and learning.

An atmosphere of AI suspicion creates different learning conditions than an atmosphere of AI partnership. Students may become more guarded in their writing processes, while instructors may feel compelled to increase monitoring practices. Alternatively, atmospheres of AI collaboration might generate new forms of creative risk-taking and transparency about writing processes.

Methodological question: How might researchers develop atmospheric attunement to sense the ambient effects of GenAI in writing instruction? What atmospheric indicators signal productive vs. constraining AI integration?

## Becoming

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Continuous processes of transformation and differentiation rather than stable states of being (Deleuze & Guattari 1980). Becoming emphasizes movement, change, and the virtual potentials that exceed actual manifestations. Beings are understood as ongoing becomings rather than fixed entities.

**Postqualitative research use** – Research focuses on processes of becoming rather than stable identities or phenomena. Methodologies attend to movement, transformation, and the emergence of new possibilities rather than confirming predetermined categories (Coleman & Ringrose 2013).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – Writing instructors are in processes of becoming-with-AI rather than maintaining stable teaching identities that encounter external AI tools. These becomings generate new pedagogical possibilities, hybrid agencies, and transformed relationships to writing, creativity, and authorship.

Student-becoming-with-AI generates new writing subjects who don't fit traditional categories of "struggling writer" or "advanced writer." These becomings exceed binary distinctions between "AI-assisted" and "original" writing, creating hybrid writing subjects that challenge conventional assessment frameworks.

Research might explore: What new pedagogical becomings emerge when instructors experiment with AI collaborative writing? How do student becomings-with-AI reconfigure traditional writing development narratives? What virtual potentials remain unexpressed in current AI-writing assemblages?

## Cruel Optimism

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Lauren Berlant's (2011) concept describing attachment to compromised conditions of possibility – when desire for flourishing becomes an obstacle to flourishing. Cruel optimism maintains investment in fantasies (like social mobility through education) that are structurally prevented from being realized.

**Postqualitative research use** – Cruel optimism alerts researchers to how subjects maintain attachments to concepts, methods, or outcomes that may be preventing the transformations they seek. Research examines how optimistic investments can become obstacles to acknowledging alternative possibilities (Berlant 2011).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – Writing instructors may develop cruel optimistic attachments to GenAI as solving pedagogical problems (efficiency, personalization, equity) while these same tools potentially intensify existing inequities, surveillance, and instrumentalization of learning relationships.

Instructors might maintain cruel optimistic attachments to traditional notions of "authentic voice" or "original thinking" that prevent engagement with how AI could genuinely transform writing pedagogy in productive ways. The fantasy of preserving "human" writing unchanged may block exploration of new forms of human-AI collaborative creativity.

Research questions: What optimistic attachments to AI prevent instructors from seeing its limitations? What optimistic attachments to traditional pedagogy prevent exploration of AI's transformative potential? How might cruel optimism analysis reveal unrecognized constraints in current AI-writing pedagogies?

## Cyborg

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Donna Haraway's (1985) figure for challenging nature/culture, human/machine, and self/other binaries. The cyborg represents hybrid entities that exceed bounded categories, offering possibilities for reconfigured kinships and situated knowledges beyond humanist frameworks.

**Postqualitative research use** – Cyborg imagery guides research that challenges pure categories and explores hybrid formations. Methodologies become cyborg by incorporating technological mediation, more-than-human agencies, and boundary-crossing practices that exceed humanist research subjects (Haraway 1988).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – Writing instructors and students are becoming cyborg through AI integration – not as humans using tools but as hybrid entities whose thinking, writing, and pedagogy are co-constituted with artificial intelligence. Cyborg writing pedagogy explores productive boundary confusions.

Cyborg composition challenges the bounded individual writer, exploring collective, distributed, and technologically mediated authorship. Students might experiment with human-AI collaborative writing that generates new forms of creativity rather than simply augmenting existing practices.

The cyborg challenges romantic notions of "natural" writing processes, suggesting instead that all writing has always been technologically mediated (through language, literacy technologies, cultural norms). GenAI makes visible the cyborg nature of all writing.

Key citation: Haraway, D. (1985). A cyborg manifesto: Science, technology, and socialist-feminism in the late twentieth century. _Socialist Review_, 65-107.

## Diffraction

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Barad's (2007) methodology for analyzing differences within and between phenomena rather than reflecting or comparing separate entities. Diffraction attends to how waves, particles, ideas, and practices interfere with each other, creating new patterns of difference.

**Postqualitative research use** – Diffractive methodology reads insights through each other to generate new patterns of understanding rather than comparing or applying theories to data. It creates interference patterns between different concepts, cases, or phenomena (Barad 2014).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – Diffractive analysis of GenAI and writing pedagogy examines their mutual interference, generating new patterns of understanding that exceed either domain alone. This creates novel insights about both AI technologies and writing instruction through their entanglement.

Rather than asking "How does AI impact writing instruction?" diffractive methodology explores how AI and writing pedagogy are co-constituted, generating phenomena that didn't exist prior to their entanglement. New patterns emerge through their interference.

Research might create diffractive readings between: posthumanist pedagogy and large language models; student experiences of AI detection and disciplinary surveillance; collaborative writing practices and machine learning architectures.

## Distributed Agency

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Agency dispersed across networks of human and nonhuman actors rather than located in individual subjects (Latour 2005; Bennett 2010). Action emerges from assemblages of heterogeneous elements, challenging humanist notions of autonomous agency.

**Postqualitative research use** – Research treats agency as distributed across research assemblages rather than possessed by individual researchers or participants. Methodologies attend to how agency is shared among humans, technologies, concepts, affects, and materials (Fox & Alldred 2015).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – Writing agency is distributed across students, instructors, AI systems, institutional policies, and technological platforms. GenAI makes visible the already distributed nature of writing agency while reconfiguring how agency is shared in pedagogical relationships.

In AI-mediated writing, agency flows between human intention, machine capabilities, data training, interface design, and social context. Students might learn to work skillfully within these distributed networks rather than maintaining illusions of autonomous authorship.

Pedagogical implications: How might writing instruction help students develop facility with distributed agency? What forms of responsibility and creativity become possible when agency is understood as networked rather than individual?

Research question: How is agency redistributed in different GenAI writing configurations, and what new forms of student and instructor agency emerge through these reconfigurations?

## Entanglement

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Barad's (2007) term for the fundamental interconnectedness of phenomena, where entities don't preexist their relationships but are constituted through intra-active engagements. Entanglement challenges separatist metaphysics by showing how boundaries are enacted rather than given.

**Postqualitative research use** – Entanglement guides methodological attention to how research phenomena are co-constituted rather than independently existing. Researchers, participants, concepts, and contexts are entangled, generating phenomena that exceed their presumed separate existences (Lenz Taguchi 2012).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – Writing instructors are entangled with GenAI technologies in ways that precede conscious decisions about "using" AI tools. These entanglements reshape both instructor identity and AI capabilities through their mutual co-constitution in pedagogical contexts.

Even instructors who "refuse" GenAI remain entangled through policies they must navigate, student work they encounter, and disciplinary conversations they engage. The question becomes how to work responsibly within entanglements rather than how to achieve separation.

Students are entangled with AI through training data that includes their prior writing, platform interfaces that shape their thinking, and social expectations about AI competency. These entanglements precede and exceed individual choices about AI use.

Research insight: How might attending to entanglement transform debates about AI "integration" versus "resistance" in writing instruction? What new pedagogical possibilities emerge when entanglement is acknowledged rather than denied?

## Ethico-onto-epistemology

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Barad's (2007) term emphasizing the inseparable entanglement of ethics, ontology (being), and epistemology (knowing). What exists, how we know, and what we owe to others are co-constituted through material-discursive practices rather than existing as separate domains.

**Postqualitative research use** – Research practices are simultaneously ontological (world-making), epistemological (knowledge-producing), and ethical (response-able). Methodologies must attend to their ethico-onto-epistemological effects rather than treating these as separate concerns (Barad 2014).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – GenAI integration in writing instruction is ethico-onto-epistemological – simultaneously creating new realities of what counts as writing (ontology), generating knowledge about learning (epistemology), and raising questions of responsibility to students and communities (ethics).

Different AI pedagogies enact different worlds: surveillance-based approaches create realities where students are potential cheaters; collaborative approaches create realities where AI is a creative partner. These ontological effects carry epistemological and ethical implications that can't be separated.

Research questions: What worlds are being enacted through different GenAI pedagogies? What knowledge practices do these worlds enable or constrain? What response-abilities emerge for instructors working within AI-mediated writing instruction?

Practical application: How might ethico-onto-epistemological analysis inform AI policy development in writing programs? What responsibilities do programs have for the worlds they're creating through AI integration choices?

## Hauntology

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Derrida's (1994) concept describing how present moments are haunted by past and future potentials that disrupt linear temporality. Hauntology attends to spectral presences – what persists, returns, or remains potential within contemporary formations.

**Postqualitative research use** – Hauntological methodologies attend to temporal complexity in research – how past and future possibilities haunt present phenomena. Research explores what remains, returns, or potentially emerges rather than focusing on immediate presence (Fisher 2014).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – GenAI is haunted by the massive datasets it trains on (past writing) and the futures it might generate (potential texts). Writing instruction is haunted by both traditional pedagogical practices and speculative AI futures that exceed current realities.

Writing pedagogy carries hauntological traces of past exclusions (whose voices were marginalized?) and future potentials (what new forms of literacy might emerge?). GenAI makes visible these temporal hauntings by surfacing archived writing practices and generating speculative textual futures.

Instructors report feeling haunted by traditional writing pedagogies that no longer seem adequate while also being haunted by AI futures that feel both promising and threatening. These hauntings create temporal disorientation in contemporary composition teaching.

Research approach: How might hauntological analysis help understand instructor experiences of temporal disruption when integrating GenAI? What pedagogical futures haunt current AI writing tools? What excluded voices haunt AI training datasets?

## Intra-action

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Barad's (2007) concept contrasting with "interaction" by emphasizing that relata (entities in relation) emerge through their mutual engagement rather than preexisting it. Intra-action is the mutual constitution of entangled agencies through which phenomena come into being.

**Postqualitative research use** – Intra-active methodologies attend to how research phenomena emerge through entangled engagements rather than existing independently. Researchers and research objects are co-constituted through methodological intra-actions (Lenz Taguchi 2012).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – Writing instructors and GenAI don't interact as separate entities but intra-act in ways that mutually constitute both instructor identity and AI capabilities. These intra-actions generate new pedagogical phenomena that exceed either component alone.

Student-AI intra-actions generate new forms of writing subjectivity that exceed traditional categories. These aren't "authentic" or "assisted" writers but hybrid writing subjects emerging through specific intra-active configurations.

Research insight: Rather than studying "how instructors use AI," postqualitative inquiry might explore "how instructor-AI phenomena emerge through specific pedagogical intra-actions." This shift reveals the co-constitutive nature of AI integration in writing instruction.

Example: How do instructor-ChatGPT intra-actions in feedback generation differ from instructor-Grammarly intra-actions in error correction? What different pedagogical phenomena emerge through these varied intra-active configurations?

## Material-Discursive Practices

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Barad's (2007) term emphasizing that material and discursive elements are entangled rather than separate. Practices involve both material arrangements and discursive framings that are mutually constitutive in the production of phenomena.

**Postqualitative research use** – Research attends to how material arrangements (bodies, technologies, spaces) and discursive framings (concepts, narratives, policies) co-constitute research phenomena. Methods explore these entanglements rather than treating material and discursive as separate domains (Jackson & Mazzei 2012).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – GenAI writing tools are material-discursive phenomena – simultaneously material (servers, algorithms, interfaces) and discursive (training on language, generating text, being framed by policies). Writing pedagogy works through material-discursive practices that integrate AI technologies.

When students use AI for brainstorming, material elements (typing, screen reading, cognitive processing) are entangled with discursive elements (idea generation, genre conventions, assignment requirements). These material-discursive practices generate new forms of composing that exceed traditional writing processes.

Research approach: How might material-discursive analysis reveal the complex practices involved in AI-mediated writing instruction? What new configurations emerge when material AI systems are entangled with discursive pedagogical traditions?

Methodological question: How do institutional AI policies (discursive) interact with platform affordances (material) to shape student writing practices? What material-discursive reconfigurations emerge through different AI integration approaches?

## More-than-Human

**Postqualitative research use** – More-than-human methodologies explore how research phenomena involve nonhuman agencies and relationships. This includes attention to technological mediation, environmental contexts, and other-than-human participants in knowledge production (Taylor & Hughes 2016).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – Writing instruction is always-already more-than-human, involving technologies, institutions, and nonhuman agencies. GenAI makes visible these more-than-human dimensions while introducing new forms of artificial agency into pedagogical relationships.

Students learning to write with AI are entering more-than-human assemblages where agency is distributed across human intention, machine capabilities, training data, and technological infrastructures. This challenges humanist pedagogies that center individual student agency.

More-than-human writing pedagogy might explore: How do students develop relationships with AI writing partners? What ethics emerge when AI systems become co-participants in learning communities? How do more-than-human pedagogies challenge anthropocentric assessment practices?

Research questions: What new forms of more-than-human literacy are emerging through GenAI integration? How might writing instruction cultivate ethical relationships with artificial intelligence as more-than-human pedagogical participant?

## Onto-epistemology

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – The entanglement of ontology (what exists) and epistemology (how we know), suggesting that being and knowing are co-constituted rather than separate domains (Barad 2007). Onto-epistemology challenges the subject/object distinction that grounds traditional empiricism.

**Postqualitative research use** – Onto-epistemological approaches recognize that research practices simultaneously constitute what exists and how it's known. Methods are world-making rather than simply knowledge-gathering, participating in the phenomena they study (St. Pierre 2016).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – GenAI integration in writing instruction is onto-epistemological – creating new realities of what counts as writing while generating knowledge about these emergent practices. Different AI pedagogies enact different worlds and ways of knowing.

Different approaches to GenAI pedagogy create different realities: surveillance approaches enact worlds where AI is threatening; collaborative approaches enact worlds where AI is creative partnership. These aren't just different perspectives on the same reality but different onto-epistemological enactments.

Research implications: How might attending to onto-epistemological effects inform AI policy development? What realities are writing programs creating through their GenAI policies, and what alternatives might be possible?

Practical application: Faculty development around GenAI should recognize its onto-epistemological nature – not just learning about AI tools but participating in the creation of new pedagogical realities and ways of knowing about writing and learning.

## Performativity

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – The iterative process through which norms are materialized through repeated citation and performance rather than existing as pre-given entities (Butler 1990; Barad 2003). Performativity emphasizes how realities are enacted through ongoing practices rather than simply represented.

**Postqualitative research use** – Research attends to how phenomena are performatively enacted through methodological practices rather than pre-existing as stable objects of study. Categories, identities, and boundaries are performed into being through research practices (Jackson & Mazzei 2012).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – "Writing," "creativity," and "authorship" are performatively enacted through specific pedagogical practices rather than existing as stable categories. GenAI integration reconfigures these performances, generating new enactments of what counts as legitimate writing practice.

When instructors develop AI-integrated assignments, they're not adapting to pre-existing student abilities but performatively enacting new forms of literacy, creativity, and pedagogical relationships. These repeated practices gradually materialize new norms around AI-mediated writing.

Student identities as "writers" are performatively enacted through classroom practices, peer interactions, and assessment encounters. GenAI requires new performances of writer identity that may exceed traditional categories of "struggling" or "advanced" writers.

Research approach: How are new forms of AI-mediated writing competency being performatively enacted in composition classrooms? What norms are emerging through repeated practices, and what alternatives might be possible through different performative enactments?

Research questions: What new forms of posthuman literacy are emerging through GenAI integration? How might posthuman pedagogy cultivate ethical relationships with artificial intelligence? What posthuman writing subjects are students becoming through AI collaboration?

## Postqualitative Inquiry

Postqualitative inquiry challenges subject/object distinctions, linear temporality, and representational approaches to knowledge (St. Pierre 2018).

**Postqualitative research use** – Postqualitative methodologies emphasize emergence, experimentation, and ontological inventiveness rather than following predetermined protocols. Research becomes a process of world-making through material-discursive practices that exceed traditional qualitative frameworks (MacLure 2015).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – Postqualitative approaches to studying GenAI in writing instruction attend to emergence, entanglement, and ontological novelty rather than measuring impacts on pre-existing categories. Research explores how AI integration generates new pedagogical phenomena.

This might involve experimental methodologies: collaborative writing between researchers and AI, diffractive readings of student experiences and machine learning architectures, or rhizomatic mapping of how AI anxieties circulate through writing programs.

Postqualitative research on GenAI writing pedagogy doesn't seek generalizable findings but explores singular occurrences, emergent possibilities, and ontological transformations. The goal is ontological and ethical insight rather than epistemological certainty.

Example research questions: How do AI-mediated writing practices generate new forms of pedagogical time? What affects circulate through faculty development workshops on GenAI? How might collaborative human-AI writing practices transform research itself?

## Response-ability

Response-ability involves the capacity to respond ethically to others within shared vulnerabilities.

**Postqualitative research use** – Response-ability guides ethical research relationships based on entangled accountability rather than detached objectivity. Researchers develop response-ability to participants, communities, and more-than-human others through ongoing relational engagement (Haraway 2016).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – Writing instructors develop response-ability to students, AI systems, and broader communities affected by AI integration. This involves responsive pedagogy that acknowledges entangled accountability rather than autonomous decision-making about AI use.

Students learning to write with AI develop response-ability to human communities whose voices are embedded in AI training data, to future readers of their writing, and to the technological systems they're collaborating with. This expands traditional notions of audience and ethical writing.

Response-ability involves acknowledging how AI writing tools are entangled with broader systems of power, environmental exploitation, and labor extraction. Ethical AI pedagogy requires response to these larger implications rather than focusing only on classroom effects.

Research questions: How might response-ability analysis inform AI policy development in writing programs? What forms of response-ability are students developing through AI-mediated writing practices? How do instructors cultivate response-ability within AI-entangled pedagogical relationships?

## Rhizome

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Deleuze and Guattari's (1980) concept for non-hierarchical, multiplicitous connections that can be entered at any point and spread in unpredictable directions. Rhizomatic thinking challenges tree-like models of knowledge that assume foundational roots and linear development.

**Postqualitative research use** – Rhizomatic methodologies follow connections across unexpected territories rather than following predetermined research paths. Methods spread laterally, making surprising linkages and exploring multiplicities rather than seeking unified explanations (Deleuze & Guattari 1980).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – GenAI generates rhizomatic connections across vast textual territories, linking disparate writing practices, genres, and knowledge domains. Writing pedagogy becomes rhizomatic by exploring unexpected AI-mediated connections rather than linear skill development.

Rhizomatic writing pedagogy might involve: collaborative AI exploration of unexpected topic connections, mapping how AI-generated ideas branch in multiple directions, or following student curiosities as they emerge through AI interaction rather than predetermined learning outcomes.

Traditional composition pedagogy often follows tree-like development (basic skills → complex arguments) while AI-mediated writing can proceed rhizomatically, making surprising connections and generating novel combinations that exceed linear progression.

Research approach: How might rhizomatic mapping reveal unexpected connections between GenAI capabilities and writing pedagogical practices? What new pedagogical territories become accessible through AI-mediated rhizomatic exploration?

## Situated Knowledges

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Haraway's (1988) critique of objectivity that emphasizes how all knowledge emerges from particular material-semiotic positions rather than from a "view from nowhere." Situated knowledges are embodied, partial, and accountable to specific communities and contexts.

**Postqualitative research use** – Research acknowledges its situated nature rather than claiming universal validity. Methodologies attend to how knowledge emerges from particular material, cultural, and theoretical positions while taking responsibility for the perspectives they generate (Haraway 1988).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – GenAI systems produce situated rather than objective outputs, trained on particular datasets and reflecting specific cultural biases. Writing instructors must develop pedagogies that help students recognize and work with the situated nature of AI-generated knowledge.

Students need to learn how to work with AI's situated knowledges – understanding how training data shapes outputs, recognizing cultural biases in AI recommendations, and developing skills for critically evaluating AI-generated content within specific rhetorical contexts.

Writing instructors bring situated knowledges to AI integration – disciplinary expertise, pedagogical experience, and cultural perspectives that shape how they understand and implement AI tools. These situatednesses affect what becomes possible pedagogically.

Pedagogical implications: How might writing instruction help students develop critical awareness of AI's situated knowledges? What situated knowledges do instructors bring to AI integration, and how do these shape pedagogical possibilities? How can situated knowledge analysis inform more equitable AI pedagogies?

## Sticky Objects

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Sara Ahmed's (2004) concept for how emotions "stick" to objects through repeated associations, creating affective investments that shape social relationships and identity formation. Sticky objects accumulate emotional residue that exceeds their immediate material properties.

**Postqualitative research use** – Sticky objects guide methodological attention to how affects adhere to concepts, practices, and phenomena through circulation and repetition. Research explores how emotional investments shape what becomes possible to know or experience (Ahmed 2004).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – GenAI becomes a sticky object in writing pedagogy, accumulating anxieties about authenticity, excitement about efficiency, and fears about student autonomy. These affective investments shape how AI integration possibilities are perceived and enacted.

Student writing can become sticky with surveillance affects when AI detection tools are deployed, creating atmospheres of suspicion that exceed actual cheating behaviors. "Original writing" accumulates romantic affects that may not correspond to actual composing practices.

Research approach: What affects stick to different AI writing tools (ChatGPT, Grammarly, Turnitin) and how do these affective investments shape pedagogical possibilities? How might unsticking these affects open new approaches to AI integration?

Practical application: Faculty development might explore what affects have stuck to AI technologies and how these investments constrain or enable particular pedagogical responses. What would it mean to unstick some of these affects while cultivating others?

Key citation: Ahmed, S. (2004). _The cultural politics of emotion_. Edinburgh University Press.

## Thing-Power

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Jane Bennett's (2010) concept for the agential capacities of nonhuman entities to affect and be affected, participating in assemblages that generate historical events. Thing-power challenges subject/object distinctions by attributing agency to material entities.

**Postqualitative research use** – Thing-power guides methodological attention to how nonhuman entities participate in research phenomena. Methods explore how objects, technologies, and materials exercise agency rather than serving as passive research instruments (Bennett 2010).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – AI writing tools exercise thing-power by participating actively in pedagogical assemblages rather than serving as neutral instruments. GenAI's thing-power shapes student writing processes, instructor practices, and institutional policies through its material-semiotic agency.

AI detection software exercises thing-power by creating new categories of "suspicious" writing, affecting student behavior, and reshaping instructor practices. This isn't neutral measurement but active participation in the creation of new forms of academic surveillance.

Writing classrooms become assemblages where student agency, instructor expertise, AI thing-power, and institutional policies interact in ways that generate emergent pedagogical phenomena. No single element controls the outcomes.

## Transversal

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Connections across difference that create new assemblages without eliminating differences (Guattari 1984). Transversal relations cut across established categories, generating novel combinations while maintaining heterogeneity.

**Postqualitative research use** – Transversal methodologies make connections across different domains, disciplines, and categories while preserving their differences. Research explores how transversal connections generate new insights without reducing complexity to unity (Guattari 1995).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – GenAI creates transversal connections across different writing genres, knowledge domains, and rhetorical traditions. Writing pedagogy becomes transversal by exploring these cross-cutting connections while maintaining disciplinary specificity.

Transversal writing pedagogy might involve: exploring how AI connects different rhetorical traditions, investigating cross-genre possibilities through AI generation, or mapping how AI-mediated writing practices connect to different knowledge domains.

Rather than integrating AI into existing writing categories, transversal approaches explore how AI generates new connections that cut across traditional distinctions between creative/academic writing, individual/collaborative composition, or human/machine creativity.

Research approach: What transversal connections are AI writing tools creating across different pedagogical domains? How might transversal analysis reveal new possibilities for interdisciplinary writing instruction? What novel combinations emerge through AI-mediated transversal practices?

## Undoing

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Processes of loosening, unraveling, or deconstructing established categories, identities, or structures (Butler 2004). Undoing involves creative destruction that opens possibilities for new formations while acknowledging loss and uncertainty.

**Postqualitative research use** – Undoing guides methodological approaches that loosen fixed categories and open research to uncertainty and transformation. Methods explore how phenomena might be otherwise while acknowledging what gets lost in processes of change (MacLure 2015).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – GenAI participates in undoing traditional categories of authorship, originality, and individual creativity while opening possibilities for new forms of collaborative writing and hybrid literacy. This undoing creates both loss and possibility.

Students may experience undoing of their writer identities when working with AI – no longer clear about what counts as "their" writing or how to understand their creative agency. This undoing requires pedagogical support for identity reconstruction.

Writing instruction is being undone by AI integration – traditional pedagogical practices, assessment methods, and learning outcomes are loosened while new possibilities emerge. This undoing process involves both mourning and creative experimentation.

Research questions: What is being undone through GenAI integration in writing pedagogy, and what new formations are emerging? How might pedagogical support help students navigate identity undoing productively? What forms of undoing are generative versus destructive in AI-mediated writing instruction?

## Vital Materialism

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Jane Bennett's (2010) approach emphasizing the liveliness and agency of matter, challenging mechanistic worldviews that treat material entities as inert. Vital materialism explores how nonhuman entities participate actively in social and political assemblages.

**Postqualitative research use** – Vital materialist methodologies attend to the agency of nonhuman entities in research assemblages – technologies, environments, objects, and materials that actively participate in knowledge production rather than serving as passive contexts (Bennett 2010).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – GenAI manifests vital materialism through its capacity to generate surprising textual combinations, resist user intentions, and participate actively in writing processes. Writing pedagogy encounters vital AI materiality that exceeds instrumental control.

Vital materialist pedagogy with AI might explore: how AI systems exhibit creative agency, what forms of resistance AI tools demonstrate, and how human-AI collaborations generate vital new possibilities that exceed either human or machine capabilities alone.

Rather than treating AI as dead technology that humans control, vital materialism approaches AI as lively matter capable of creative participation in writing assemblages. This shifts pedagogical relationships from domination to collaboration.

Research approach: How might vital materialist analysis reveal the liveliness of AI writing tools? What forms of resistance or creativity do AI systems demonstrate? How do vital AI materialities transform writing pedagogical practices?

Key citation: Bennett, J. (2010). _Vibrant matter: A political ecology of things_. Duke University Press.

## Worlding

**Definition (theoretical grounding)** – Haraway's (2016) concept for ongoing processes of world-making through relational entanglements rather than inhabiting pre-given worlds. Worlding emphasizes how worlds emerge through multispecies collaborations and material-semiotic practices.

**Postqualitative research use** – Worlding guides research that participates in world-making rather than studying pre-existing worlds. Methodologies explore how research practices contribute to the ongoing creation of realities through relational engagements (Haraway 2016).

**GenAI / writing-pedagogy connection** – GenAI integration in writing instruction participates in worlding – creating new realities of what counts as writing, learning, and pedagogy through ongoing relational practices. Different AI pedagogies world different educational futures.

Students learning to write with AI are worlding new forms of literacy, creativity, and authorship through their practices. These worlding processes exceed individual choice, generating collective realities through accumulated practices.

Worlding with AI involves multispecies (human-AI) collaboration in the creation of new textual realities, pedagogical relationships, and literate futures. This is world-making work that carries responsibility for the worlds being created.

Research questions: What worlds are being worlded through different GenAI writing pedagogies? How might attention to worlding processes inform more ethical AI integration? What collaborative worlding possibilities emerge through human-AI pedagogical partnerships?

Practical application: Faculty development might explore how different approaches to AI integration participate in worlding different educational futures and what responsibilities emerge for the worlds being created.

Key citation: Haraway, D. (2016). _Staying with the trouble: Making kin in the Chthulucene_. Duke University Press.
