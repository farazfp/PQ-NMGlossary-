

> [!quote]
> Affect is the name we give to those forces—visceral forces beneath, alongside, or generally other than conscious knowing, vital forces insisting beyond emotion—that can serve to drive us toward movement, toward thought and extension, that can likewise suspend us (as if in neutral) across a barely registering accretion of force-relations, or that can even leave us overwhelmed by the world’s apparent intractability.
>
> _— Gregg, M., & Seigworth, G. J., 2010, p. 1_

#### Definition

In the context of posthumanist and new materialist inquiry, [[Affect]] refers to the body's capacity to affect and be affected. It is a visceral, pre-personal force or intensity that emerges in the encounters between bodies, objects, technologies, and ideas. It is the energetic charge that precedes and exceeds conscious cognition and named [[Emotion]]. [[Affect]] is the body’s autonomic response to a situation—the quickening pulse, the tightening in the chest, the flush of the skin—that occurs before the mind has had a chance to process, categorize, and label the experience with a socio-linguistic concept like "fear" or "excitement." It is a continuous, unfolding process of becoming, a modulation of a body's power to act and engage with the world. This understanding treats [[Affect]] as fundamentally relational and trans-subjective; it circulates within and through an [[Assemblage]], rather than originating from or belonging to an individual subject. Affect is what makes things matter, what gives them their pull, and what constitutes the very fabric of experience and relation; It shifts the analytical focus toward the dynamic and relational field of forces in which subjects and objects emerge, rather than seeing them as pre-existing entities who then interact.

#### Key Literature and Scholars

The philosophical lineage of [[Affect]] is often traced to Baruch Spinoza's (1677/1996) _Ethics_, where he defines it as the modifications of the body by which that body's power of acting is increased or diminished. This Spinozist foundation was powerfully re-energized by Gilles Deleuze and Félix Guattari (1987), who positioned [[Affect]] as a central component of their nomadic philosophy, emphasizing its nature as a mobile, non-human intensity that passes between bodies. Brian Massumi (2002) provided a highly influential articulation for contemporary theory, drawing a critical distinction between [[Affect]] as unqualified, autonomic intensity and [[Emotion]] as the qualified, narrativized capture of that intensity. Sara Ahmed (2004) further developed the concept through her work on [[Affective Economies]], showing how affects "stick" to certain bodies and signs, circulating in ways that shape social and political worlds.Similarly, Kathleen Stewart (2007) champions a methodological attunement to "ordinary affects," the subtle yet powerful intensities that compose the texture of everyday life and give shape to public feelings. These scholars, among others, have established affect as a crucial concept for understanding the world as a dynamic field of relational forces.

#### Relevance to Research Questions

[[Affect]] is directly relevant to the core inquiries of this research. The project's second research question, which investigates the "current anxieties about GenAI," can be productively re-framed through an affective lens. The term "anxiety" can be understood as a circulating, collective [[Affect]] within the educational field, produced by the [[Intra-action]] of media reports, institutional policies, student practices, and the material presence of [[Generative AI]] itself. This approach allows the inquiry to trace how this visceral intensity is then channeled and narrativized into specific, named [[Emotions]] like fear, panic, or excitement, as explored in the first research question. Furthermore, addressing the third research question regarding the instructor's role, an affective perspective helps to re-conceptualize pedagogy. The instructor becomes an affective body, part of a larger learning [[Assemblage]], whose role involves attuning to and modulating the [[Affective Atmospheres]] of the classroom to foster certain capacities for learning and engagement.

By focusing on affect, the research can explore how these anxieties are produced, how they move, and what they do in the world—how they shape pedagogical practices, influence policy decisions, and contribute to the formation of dominant narratives about technology and education. It moves the analysis from what people think about AI to how the educational [[assemblage]] is being affectively composed and recomposed by its introduction.
#### Connections to Other Concepts

[[Affect]] is inextricably entangled with other key concepts. It is the energetic current that flows through and helps constitute any given [[Assemblage]], giving it a particular dynamic quality. This relationality and flow is generated through the process of [[Intra-action]], where all elements of the assemblage—human and non-human—mutually constitute one another in an ongoing material-discursive process, where affect is understood as emerging from within encounters, not as a pre-existing property of any single entity. [[The Body]] is the primary site for registering affect, acting as a sensor for the intensities that traverse a situation, which aligns with a [[New Materialism]] that takes the material world seriously. 

While related, [[Affect]] is conceptually distinct from [[Emotion]]; [[Emotion]] can be understood as the capture, naming, and socialization of a prior, more primary affective intensity. A focus on [[Affect]] is a hallmark of [[New Materialism]], as it takes seriously [[The Body]] and its capacities as central to knowing and being. The collective circulation of affects within a given environment creates what can be termed [[Affective Atmospheres]]. Methodologically, tracing these currents requires an [[Affective Method]], and a [[Diffraction]]-based analysis can read how affects from one historical moment resonate through and are reconfigured in another.

#### Methodological Implications

A theoretical commitment to [[Affect]] demands a corresponding methodological orientation. An [[Affective Method]] is required, one that cultivates a sensitivity to the intensities, rhythms, and atmospheres of a research site. This requires the researcher to become a carefully attuned instrument, sensitive to the atmospheric and visceral dimensions of the research context and moves the research beyond a reliance on purely representational data, such as interview transcripts, which primarily capture [[Emotion]] and narrative. It calls for ethnographic techniques that attend to the non-discursive: data collection expands to include mood, rhythm, bodily comportment, tone of voice, spatial arrangements, and the overall "feel" of a room or space where AI is being discussed or used. 

The analysis focuses on tracing the circulation of intensities and identifying how affect sticks to certain words, ideas, or bodies, and the researcher's own affective responses become a crucial source of data, a resonant means for sensing the affective field of the inquiry. Writing, too, becomes a methodological concern, aiming to evoke the affective field and qualities, and the felt sense of the research encounter for the reader, rather than simply reporting on them from an objective distance.

#### Example from the Field

Consider a university-mandated faculty workshop on implementing a new AI policy. An affective analysis would attend to the palpable intensity in the room. It would note the collective sense of unease when the presenter mentions that "we don't know how they embed what they know." It would describe the way the phrase "academic integrity" seems to hang in the air, charged with a history of moral and ethical weight. It would observe the mixture of postures—some instructors leaning forward with keen interest, others slumped back with visible exhaustion or resistance. It would describe the palpable mixture of excitement and dread in the room—an affective atmosphere that is neither purely positive nor negative, but a complex field of potential. This circulating, shared intensity is the [[affect]] of the event, which precedes and informs any individual instructor's later articulation of feeling "anxious" or "excited." The analysis would trace the circulation of this tense, ambivalent [[Affect]] as it moves through the room, sticking to certain slides, phrases, and bodies, shaping the potential for what can be said, thought, and felt in that pedagogical [[Assemblage]].

#### Critiques and Limitations

The concept of [[Affect]] is not without its challenges. A primary critique, forcefully articulated by Leys (2011), centers on the epistemological difficulty of researching a phenomenon defined as pre-personal and non-conscious. If [[Affect]] precedes language and cognition, any attempt to write about or analyze it necessarily transforms it into the very [[Emotion]] or representation from which it is distinguished. This methodological paradox, as Leys and others argue, can lead to analyses that are speculative or risk imposing the researcher's own affective interpretations. 

Echoing this concern, Aubrey Anable (2018) has criticized some affect theorists for an imprecision that gives their work a "maddening incoherence" that can "shade too easily into purely subjective responses to the world" [Source: Affect theory - Wikipedia]. Furthermore, some scholars caution against a romanticization of [[Affect]] as a pure, authentic, or politically progressive force that exists outside of power. As Sara Ahmed's (2004) work shows, affects are deeply implicated in social norms and power structures, often serving to reinforce existing hierarchies. A critical application of the concept must remain attentive to how affects are mobilized, channeled, and put to work within specific political and social contexts.

### [[Affect]]
- **Core Definition:** Affect is understood not as a personal feeling or emotion, but as a pre-personal, non-conscious **intensity** that arises in the "in-between" of encounters. It is the body's capacity *to affect and be affected* (Deleuze & Guattari, 1987). It represents a primary, visceral force that precedes and exceeds conscious awareness and subjective feeling.
	- Brian Massumi (2002) famously distinguishes affect from [[Emotion]] by describing a "half-second lag" between the body's autonomic, affective reaction to a stimulus and the conscious brain's processing and naming of that reaction as a specific emotion. Affect is that raw, unqualified intensity in the lag time.
	- It is fundamentally relational and trans-personal; it does not reside within a subject but circulates within an [[assemblage]] of bodies, objects, technologies, and discourses.
- **Key Characteristics:**
	- **Intensity, not Quality:** Affect is pure intensity. [[Emotion]] is the capture and qualification of that intensity, giving it a name (e.g., "joy," "anger") and fitting it into a personal or social [[narrative]].
	- **Autonomic and Visceral:** Affect is registered by the body at a level beneath conscious thought. It is the blush, the quickening heart rate, the knot in the stomach. This connects it deeply to [[The Body]] as a site of knowing and being, central to [[New Materialism]].
	- **Force and Capacity:** Following Spinoza, affect is the force that increases or decreases a body's power of acting. It is what moves a body, what compels it toward or away from other bodies and objects. It is a capacity that emerges through [[intra-action]].
	- **Atmospheric:** Affects can be collective and environmental. One can speak of an "affective atmosphere" of a classroom, a meeting, or a historical moment. Sara Ahmed (2004) explores how affects are not free-floating but "stick" to certain bodies and signs, creating [[Affective Economies]].
- **Distinction from [[Emotion]]:**
	- **Affect:**
		- Pre-personal, non-conscious.
		- Bodily, autonomic intensity.
		- Unformed and unqualified.
		- A state of relationality and [[entanglement]].
		- Example: The physical jolt and heightened senses when hearing an unexpected sound in the dark.
	- **Emotion:**
		- Personal, subjective.
		- The socio-linguistic naming and framing of an affect.
		- Tied to cognition, memory, and [[narrative]].
		- Often understood within established psychological or cultural scripts.
		- Example: Naming the jolt as "fear" and connecting it to a story: "I am scared because I am alone."
- **Connections to the Research:**
	- **On [[Anxiety]] and [[Generative AI]]:** The project can frame anxiety not as an individual instructor's psychological state *about* AI, but as a collective and circulating affect within the educational field. This affect is produced by the [[intra-action]] of policy documents, media hype, student practices, institutional pressures, and the non-human [[agency]] of the AI itself. The research can then trace how this raw, circulating affect gets captured and narrativized into specific emotions like "fear of replacement" or "anxiety about cheating."
	- **On [[Pedagogy]]:** An affective lens shifts the focus of pedagogy from what instructors think or believe to the affective atmosphere of the learning [[assemblage]]. How does the [[intra-action]] of instructor-student-AI produce certain capacities for action and foreclose others? What affects are generated in these encounters, and how do they enable or constrain learning? This aligns with a [[Post-qualitative Inquiry]] that is interested in what is *produced* in an inquiry event.
	- **On [[Ethics]]:** An ethics informed by affect theory is less about pre-determined rules or principles and more about attunement to the capacities being produced in a situation. An ethical response is one that fosters "good" affects—those that increase a body's (individual or collective) capacity to act and flourish (Deleuze & Guattari, 1987). This provides a way to think about the ethics of AI in education beyond simple pro/con debates.
- **Open Questions for the Work:**
	- How do dominant [[narratives]] about AI function as machines for channeling and organizing affect?
	- What are the specific material-discursive practices (e.g., workshop designs, university policies, user interface designs) that generate particular affects around [[Generative AI]] in higher education?
	- If affect is pre-personal, how can one methodologically "tune in" to the affective currents of a research site? What does it mean to research affect rather than [[emotion]]?
