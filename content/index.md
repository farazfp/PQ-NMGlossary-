# Introduction
  
  This page establishes a mapping between the rich interdisciplinary vocabulary of New Materialism, Posthumanism and a research focus on the entanglement of Generative AI, technology, writing, and pedagogy in higher education. By engaging with these concepts in a postqualitative research orientation, the mapping seeks to illuminate how human and nonhuman agencies converge, whether in the affective experiences emerging from GenAI/LLM interactions, the cyborgification of modern writing practices, or in innovative pedagogical strategies. In each case, it is intended to provide a foundation for extrapolating further theoretical development and guiding empirical inquiry.

### [[Affect]]

Affect refers to the pre-personal, non-conscious intensities and forces that move through and between bodies, technologies, and environments, shaping what becomes possible in research and pedagogical encounters. In postqualitative and new materialist inquiry, affect is not reducible to emotion or individual feeling; rather, it is understood as a relational, trans-subjective force that circulates within assemblages, modulating capacities for action and engagement. <details>   <summary>💡More context</summary> In the context of GenAI in higher education, affect is central to understanding how anxieties, excitements, and uncertainties are collectively produced and circulated as instructors and students encounter new technological possibilities and constraints. </details>
### [[Affective Method]]
  
  The Affective Method centers on understanding how emotions and embodied responses are integral to material-discursive practices. It designates a research orientation that foregrounds attunement to the intensities, atmospheres, and resonances that shape research events and pedagogical practice. Rather than seeking to represent or categorize emotions, affective method involves tracing how [[#Affect|affect]] moves, sticks, and transforms within [[#Assemblages Assembling the Unassembled|assemblages]] of bodies, technologies, and discourses. <details>   <summary>💡More context</summary>   This approach is particularly salient for research on GenAI in higher education, as it enables the exploration of how new technologies reconfigure the affective atmospheres of classrooms, faculty meetings, and institutional life, often in ways that exceed what can be captured through traditional qualitative methods. In the context of GenAI/LLM-enhanced writing, instructors encounter affective engagements, ranging from excitement at innovative possibilities to anxiety about algorithmic judgments. This concept suggests that curricular designs and feedback protocols can incorporate emotional literacy, thereby foregrounding the visceral, affect-laden aspects of technological entanglements. </details>

### Affirmative Difference
  
  Affirmative Difference emphasizes recognizing and valorizing differences without reducing them to oppositional binaries. <details>   <summary>💡More context</summary>Within cyborg writing practices, this perspective invites an exploration of the creative frictions between human intuition and algorithmic processes. It encourages a pedagogy that does not simply reconcile these differences but rather affirms them as productive sites for generating innovative, hybrid modes of knowledge production.</details>

### Agency
  
  Agency is the enactment of effects in the world: the situated doing/being that takes form in specific material-discursive arrangements, rather than a property that entities possess. It names the capacity for differential [[#Becoming|becoming]] that arises as human, nonhuman, material, and discursive bodies, technologies, spaces, and discourses [[#Intra-action|intra-act]], producing particular possibilities while excluding others. Defined this way, agency is eventful, relational, and always specific to and distributed throughout the [[#Entanglement|entangled]] [[#apparatus|apparatus]] that brings it forth; it is *not* intention, will, or control, but the concrete forces that make some actions, meanings, and bodies matter here-and-now.   <details>   <summary>💡More context</summary>    In the context of GenAI and writing instruction, agency is distributed across assemblages and coordinated intra-actions of instructors, students, language, algorithms, prompts, interfaces, policies, and digital infrastructures. For example, a prompt template, an LMS integration, and an assessment rubric together enable certain textual moves while foreclosing others. This definition reframes authorship and responsibility as effects of these arrangements, highlighting how agency is distributed, negotiated, and accountable within pedagogical assemblages, where boundaries between subjects and objects are continually negotiated and reconfigured. </details>
   
### Agential Cut
  
  The Agential Cut refers to the moment when boundaries are drawn, enabling the distinction and interrelation of entities within an assemblage. <details>   <summary>💡More context</summary>Applied to a postqualitative approach in GenAI environments, instructors might use this notion to critically examine how delineations between the organic (human) and the inorganic (machine) are constituted. This also raises questions on the limits of responsibility and authorship, suggesting new conventions for collaborative creation.</details>

#### Agential Realism

Agential realism, developed by Karen Barad, is a theoretical framework that dissolves the boundaries between ontology (being), epistemology (knowing), and ethics. It posits that entities do not pre-exist their relations but emerge through [[#Intra-action|intra-actions]] , or mutual, constitutive encounters that produce both subjects and objects. In this view, agency is not an attribute of individuals but an enactment distributed across human and nonhuman actors, including technologies like GenAI. <details>   <summary>💡More context</summary>    Agential realism is particularly relevant for research on AI in education, as it provides tools for analyzing how knowledge, matter, and meaning are co-produced in the ongoing entanglement of instructors, students, algorithms, and institutional apparatuses. </details>

### Becoming

Becoming is a central concept in new materialist and posthumanist thought, emphasizing process, transformation, and the ongoing emergence of identities, entities and identities. Rather than viewing subjects or objects as fixed and stable, becoming foregrounds the continual, fluid, contingent, and relational nature of existence. <details>   <summary>💡More context</summary>In the context of GenAI and writing instruction, both instructors and students are understood as always in the process of becoming, shaped by their  [[#Entanglement|entanglements]] with technologies, institutional structures, and each other. ### Becoming-with
  Becoming-with is a concept that foregrounds the inherently relational, co-constitutive, and entangled nature of transformation, emphasizing that change and emergence always occur in concert with others- human, nonhuman, material, and technological. Unlike the more general notion of becoming, which highlights ongoing process, transformation, and the fluidity of identity or matter, becoming-with insists that such processes are never solitary or isolated. Instead, they are always shaped through companionship, collaboration, and [[#Intra-action|intra-act]] with other bodies, entities, and forces. <details>   <summary>💡More context</summary>In the context of GenAI in higher education writing instruction, becoming-with describes how instructors, students, and AI systems are mutually transformed through their ongoing engagements, each shaping and being shaped by the other. This concept resists individualistic or anthropocentric accounts of change, instead attending to the ways in which subjectivities, practices, and knowledge are co-produced in the thick of relational entanglement. Becoming-with thus offers a nuanced lens for understanding the distributed, more-than-human dynamics of pedagogical and creative processes in digitally mediated educational assemblages. </details>

### Creativity

Creativity denotes the event of bringing forth novel and fitting differences within a situated assemblage, where “novel” names an emergent variation that did not previously exist in that configuration, and “fitting” marks its consequential uptake—how it reconfigures capacities, relations, and possibilities for action. In a postqualitative and new materialist register, creativity is not a property of individuals but a process of transduction across materials, bodies, discourses, and technologies that transforms constraints into affordances and stabilizes invention into practice. It emerges from the entangled relations among humans, technologies, materials, and discourses. <details>   <summary>💡More context</summary>Within GenAI‑entangled writing instruction, creativity appears when human and algorithmic operations co-compose problem framings, generate unexpected moves (recombination, reframing, translation across modalities), and sediment these into pedagogical routines, genres, or tools that expand what writing can do. This understanding distinguishes creativity from originality (authorship as ownership) and productivity (more of the same), emphasizing instead situated emergence, evaluative fit to context, and the increased capacity of the assemblage to think, feel, and act differently. Creativity is distributed and co-constituted by both human and nonhuman actors, including algorithms, prompts, and institutional expectations. This perspective challenges traditional hierarchies that privilege human originality, instead highlighting the generative potential of collaborative and more-than-human creative processes that unfold within educational assemblages. </details>

### Diffraction
  
  Diffraction names a specifically onto-epistemological practice, after Haraway and Barad, for making differences-that-matter perceptible by reading phenomena “through” one another to map patterns of interference, rather than mirroring or representing them as already separate. As method and metaphor, it treats knowing as part of world-making: phenomena, researchers, and apparatuses co-constitute what becomes visible through agential cuts. Diffractive analysis follows the consequences of cuts in situated assemblages, attending to exclusions, resonances, and ethical accountabilities that reconfigure pedagogy, authorship, and agency as more-than-human practices. As a method, it moves beyond simple cause-and-effect analysis, instead focusing on the complex, generative, and often unpredictable effects that arise from the entanglement of matter and meaning in educational assemblages.
  
Unlike reflection, which seeks to mirror or represent a pre-existing reality, diffraction attends to the patterns of difference, interference, and entanglement that emerge when phenomena inter-act . Reading diffractively involves bringing multiple theories, data sources, or historical moments into contact not to compare them, but to see what new insights or patterns are generated in their encounter. <details>   <summary>💡More context</summary>In the context of GenAI in higher education, and in AI-entangled writing instruction, diffraction involves staging careful comparative encounters—such as policy texts through classroom practices, media narratives through instructors’ fieldnotes, or LLM interface affordances through assessment rubrics—to trace how boundaries like author/tool, cheating/learning, or creativity/derivation are enacted and with what effects. This approach allows for an analysis that reads current anxieties about AI through past technological panics, tracing how they interfere and produce new affective formations. Similarly, it enables a reading of pedagogical practices, student writing, and algorithmic outputs through one another to understand how they are co-constituted.  </details>
